Microptimizations

28 Jun. 2015

I very much enjoy browsing other developer's blogs and personal websites to absorb as much information as I can. I know I'm a long way from being a great developer so I always look for interesting and easy ways to improve and sharpen my skills. Since starting as an Extreme Blue intern, I've had much greater flexibility when it comes to coding style. Last summer, my mentor in CommServer was excellent at reviewing my code and giving me targeted feedback. This summer, though our technical mentors review our code, they focus more on architecture rather than style and efficiency. They've let us run with our ideas without much supervision.

I see this as a blessing and a curse. I thrive under the tuteledge of mentors and getting any sort of feedback on my work. Criticism and editing are vital components of the writing process (as I noted in my rapid reactions to finishing my thesis) and I believe they are also vital aspects to the development process. As a software developer, I firmly believe no code is perfect after a first pass. I often note methods and classes for revision and search for ways to tweak and tune my code to be faster or more readable. This summer, without the sctrict, watchful eye of a mentor, I have to largely trust myself to review and test my own code.

I came across a well maintained site of a C++ developer by the name of Constantine Tarasenkov. The UI of the website was very clean so I stuck around and came across this post, which featured a few videos from CppCon 2014. Though my C++ chops are far from professional, I nonetheless watched a good portion of the first video. The most useful advice I took away came around 26 minutes, where Chandler points out the very simple idea of only making one call to retrieve the value of the cache once. This was a forehead-smacking moment for me.
