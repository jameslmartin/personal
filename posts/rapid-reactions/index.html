Rapid reactions on finishing a thesis

15 Apr. 2015

It's been almost two months since my last post. What have I been up to?

The months of February and March are largely a blur. I'm sure when April is finished, it too will have been a blur. Penning the experiences of my final semester of undergraduate studies would be a massive undertaking, so instead I want to discuss some key sections of my research, some problems I faced, and my reflections on the work I produced. Here are some of my rapid reactions to finishing my thesis:

  • A senior Honors Thesis is a hefty undertaking in context of finishing classes, social pressures, and of course - figuring out what the hell you're planning on doing with the rest of your life. I spent countless nights tucked away in some corner of Davis Library, hunched over my laptop, tapping out lines of code for some graph or scratching my head thinking man, this paragraph I wrote sounds awfully repetitive. I also found myself ditching other work to tinker with the data. My thesis became somewhat of an obsession and I easily spent between 20-30 hours a week working on it.

  • It's better to have a complete body of work that is not perfect than not have a completed body of work at all. Finishing something, whether it was finally setting up a well-functioning coding environment, a section of a paper, or a presentation, is vital to the next step in the research process...

  • ...which is editing. I love editing. Taking a red pen to something I finished is a magical feeling. Tweaking, crafting, rewording, refining bodies of work is so incredibly important to finding your voice. I talk to so many of my peers and hear they often turn in completely raw, unedited first drafts of papers of sizable length. I can't help but cringe.

  • By the same token, no body of work can ever be 100% perfect. I've learned over my past four years of college that no program, no paper, no assignment that has any sort of subjective component to it, can ever be 100% perfect. I could sit and rewrite my thesis for weeks, but at some point I have to stop myself and say, I've done all I can, and I am satisfied with this result. This doesn't necessarily mean you have to settle for less, it just means you can't beat yourself up over the process. At some point you have to deliver a product. As my roommate says, It has to go 1.0 sometime.

  • Massaging data isn't always easy. I find sample data (see Hack Night Demo) all the time with no holes or NaNs and nice, neat columns. In reality, data can be messy. One of the biggest challenges I faced at the start of joining the net-score project was taking seven months of data and combining and storing it into a single place. Luckily, I found pandas which single handedly enabled my work to go so much smoother. I first started out by concatenating CSV files using cat and piping the output to a new file. After wanting to pull my hair out for an admittedly longer period of time than necessary, and taking winter break to mess with pandas, I managed to neatly combine all of our data into a single DataFrame.

  • An Honors Thesis is a great first foray into academic research. I learned how to handle the amount of freedom researchers are given. Asking and answering questions are up to the individual, and I found myself with the ability to steer the project in whatever direction I wished. However, having a mentor to guide my thought process helped me the most.

In the coming weeks, I hope to publish parts of my thesis on this website. I have a lot of great plots, charts, and maps that really dive and provide insights into the data that the net-score project collected over a short deployment. Stay tuned!
